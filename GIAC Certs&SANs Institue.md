### Foundational Skills, Four GIAC Certifications, and a Solid Step Forward  

## TL;DR  

- The SANS Applied Cybersecurity (ACS) Certificate Program helps students gain practical, career-focused cybersecurity skills.  
- It includes four GIAC certifications: GFACT, GSEC, GCIH, plus one elective (mine was GWAPT).  
- Expect about two to three months per certification if you study steadily.  
- A major key to success is mastering the index and studying all course material. Just completing labs or videos may not be enough.

## My Experience with the ACS Program  

After completing my bachelor‚Äôs degree, I wanted to strengthen my technical foundation and gain more hands-on, industry-relevant experience in cybersecurity. The ACS program from SANS was a good match for that goal.  

The program is designed for students who want to upskill or transition into cybersecurity, and it is structured around four GIAC certifications. Three are required and one is an elective based on your interests:

| Certification | Focus Area |

| **GSEC** | Enterprise security essentials and applied defensive practices |

I completed each certification in roughly one to one and a half months, which kept pacing high but manageable. Having a consistent study rhythm helped keep the learning fresh and moving forward.

## Study Cadence and Strategy  

Here is what worked for me across all four certifications:

- I treated each course like a mini-program, dedicating focused weeks for intense study and lab work.  
- I prioritized labs and hands-on exercises more than just watching lectures.  
- I used active highlighting and indexing to retain and reference concepts.  
- I reviewed earlier material each week instead of relying on last-minute cramming.  

That consistent rhythm helped me transition smoothly between certifications and build cumulative knowledge.

## Indexing and Deep Study Strategy  

A major differentiator in my success was how I approached the indexing and deep study of the course books and materials.

### Why Index  

GIAC exams are open-book and open-note, and they reward candidates who have a well-organized reference system at hand. One of the best guides is an article by Lesley Carhart, also known as Hacks4Pancakes, titled ‚ÄúBetter GIAC Testing with Pancakes.‚Äù It outlines how indexing the books and creating tabbed systems can help both learning and exam performance.  
[Read the article here](https://tisiphone.net/2015/08/18/giac-testing/)

### My Indexing Process  

- I used an Excel spreadsheet with two columns. The first column contained the book number and page number (for example, ‚Äú1.100‚Äù = Book 1, page 100). The second column listed the chapter or specific topic referenced.  
- Each book was color-coded with a distinct tab, and within the book, each chapter or section had its own color tab for quick navigation.  
- When studying videos or course material, I had the book open and indexed live as I followed along. This helped me focus on key tools, definitions, commands, and facts without over-highlighting.  
- My index included tools, definitions, commands, and concept keywords, but I avoided highlighting everything. The goal was precision and usability, not quantity.  
- Regularly reviewing the index and the tabbed books reinforced memory retention and allowed fast lookups during the exam.  

By indexing thoroughly and staying engaged with the material, I gained two major benefits: deeper understanding during study and faster referencing during exams.
